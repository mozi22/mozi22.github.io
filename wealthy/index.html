How to break into top 1% with cryptocurrencies

Giving oneself ambitious goals has always been an effective strategy for faster progress. Since almost everyone in today’s world is looking for smart ways to accumulate wealth, here we dig down deeper on how cryptocurrencies can be leveraged to break into the top 1% of ones own country.

Every country has it’s own threshold of wealth that one needs to acquire to break into the top 1% and these thresholds are calculated based on the different wealth measurement models proposed by different companies or organizations around the world. The models take into account different economic variables to reach a final estimation of how much wealth flows within the country directly impacting the economic well being of its inhabitants and in turn, the threshold above which 1% population resides. This also means, you need to be worth comparatively less to be among the top 1% of less economically stable countries than the more stable ones.

For example, the model proposed by Knight Frank for wealth sizing was calculated by using a combination of previous research from (Davies et al. (2017)) taking household wealth distribution from 33 countries into account to estimate the country wide wealth distribution. These wealth distribution data came primarily from the household balance sheets (HBS) in addition to other financial and non financial wealth data provided by most countries. Econometric and other statistical estimation techniques like Gini coefficients were employed to attain a wealth distribution for individual countries. The final distribution curve attained helped in division of individuals into three different wealth categories for the location i.e

  • HNWIs (high net worth individuals) — those with net wealth of $1 million or more
  • UHNWIs (ultra-high net worth individuals) — those with net wealth of $30 million or more
  • billionaires — those with net wealth of $1 billion or more.

This explains the division of wealth among the people but doesn’t answer how much wealth is required to be among the top 1%. To understand how thresholds are calculated, imagine the country’s total wealth to be distributed among the population as 100%. Different portions of this 100% will be received by different percent of the population (e.g 5% of the population is earning a slice of 10% of the total wealth which leaves 90% of the wealth to be distributed among the remaining 95% of the population ). The top 1% is usually owning a bigger slice of that 100%, let’s say 30%. Simply dividing the wealth representing 30% (for e.g $100 billion) with the number of people representing the percentage of population (1% equivalent to 10,000 families) results in the threshold value.($10 million in this case).

Interestingly the thresholds calculated by Knight Frank are far lower than their calculated UHNWIs wealth. The country with the richest people in the world is Monaco and a threshold of only $7.9 million is required to be in that margin which sets up the goal of owning 213.2 BTC to break into the top 1%. But if someone owns only half of BTC, this already counts the person among the richest 1% people in Kenya. Since owning a single BTC is not easy due to high prices there are other coins like polkadot and BNB that are still not out of grasp. During May 2021 the price of one BNB coin has been around $337.69 which is a 1872.48% increase from May 2020 price of $17.12. If even less than half of the given rate is considered (i.e 900%) per year, it can take BNB around 2 years and some months to reach equivalent to the price of BTC which means one can already leave behind the richest people of several countries.
